Imagine you're a biologist in the heart of a lush and tangled jungle, searching for endangered birds. All of a sudden, you catch a glimpse of a great shadow illuminated against the treeline. It may be the bird you're searching for, but there's no way of knowing just from the sliver of it's silhouette you are able to see. You pull out your notebook and quickly sketch what you can see, a wing, maybe even a claw? but just as you finish sketching you look up to the treeline, and see the bird has disappeared.
Hours later, you manage to track the bird down, but once again you still can only see a fragment of it's shadow, but now from a different angle. After many such encounters, each revealing a different piece of the puzzle, you’ve finally gathered enough sketches to deduce the bird’s full form, even though you’ve never once seen it in its entirety. This, is the essence of quantum state tomography.

The quantum world behaves very differently from the world you and I are used to. Take a computer bit for instance, we know the bit can either be "on" (1) or "off" (0). If we knew everything regarding the system (the circuit) - whether a current is running, the voltage, the circuit's wiring — we could predict the state of the bit with absolute certainty. We say this system is deterministic.

In the quantum realm, however, our classical intuition regarding predicting the state of a system completely crumbles to pieces. Even if we had all the possible information regarding the circuit, we would still be uncertain regarding the state of the bit (whether it's 0 or 1). We say the bit is actually in a superposition of both 0 and 1, and we could only specify the probability of realising each. This uncertainty does not represent uncertainty regarding the system, and rather is fundamental to quantum mechanics and the universe! 

This is what makes the task of quantum state tomography so vital. If one was to measure such a system once, you may get a single outcome, but now how are you supposed to know what the mix of probabilities was before you measured? Quantum state tomography aims to provide an informed answer to this question, and starts by preparing multiple identical copies of the quantum system, and then repeatedly performing multiple measurements in the different system bases — essentially looking at the system from a variety of angles.

To illustrate, imagine a world-renowned pastry chef hosting a game show. The challenge? Recreate their secret carrot cake recipe. The twist? The chef has made a hundred identical cakes, but each contestant is only allowed to taste a single crumb from one cake at a time — and thanks to a mischievous wizard's curse, the cake vanishes as soon as they take a bite. Still, over time and many crumbs later, the contestants begin to gather enough information. A hint of cinnamon here, a touch of vanilla icing there — until finally, they can reconstruct the exact recipe without ever tasting a full slice. That’s the essence of quantum state tomography: piecing together a hidden whole from vanishing fragments.

In more technical terms, we perform multiple measurements in the different bases of the system, in order to reconstruct the density matrix $\rho$ of the system. This matrix encodes all the system's information, it's probabilities, coherences, and uncertainties. Reconstructing this matrix can be a painstaking process for physicists, however, allows us to peer behind the curtains and into the mysterious and strange quantum realm.

At first this may just seem like an obscure process for the laboratory, however, it is crucial for the assurance of reliable and effective quantum computing hardware. Consider a quantum computer, which performs actions on a quantum (rather than classical) state, and is able to perform tasks no classical computer ever could. One major problem is, how do we know the quantum computer is doing what we expect it should? If one receives just a single measurement outcome, all bets are off as to what the true state was before measuring. Quantum state tomography lets us peel back the curtain, as upon performing many measurements, one is able to make a confident statement regarding the state of the system prior to measurement.

The field of quantum computing is skyrocketing, with every passing year the number of quantum bits keep increasing. As these systems linearly increase in the quantum bit count, the number of possible states the system can realise increases exponentially. This is a double edged sword, as although it is the fundamental reason why quantum computers are so effective, it is also why they are so difficult to control.

This has brought upon an exiting intersection between data science and physics within the field of quantum state tomography, due to the fact that we need more efficient and elegant methods for reconstructing the state of a system as they grow exponentially larger. It’s akin to trying to reconstruct a Michelangelo sculpture from just it's shadows, where every extra centimetre of sculpture demands hundreds more angles.

Data science focuses on making intelligent data-driven predictions regarding the state of the world, and employs advanced algorithms to "learn" how to accurately make predictions based on available data. The issue with only utilising this approach, is that it neglects any of the physical laws in place which the universe follows (such as the laws of quantum physics), and purely focuses on fitting the model according to data. The synergy between the two fields occurs, when data science is able to bake in the knowledge about the physical world into their model's learning algorithm. These models may be able to punish predictions which violate reality, and rewards ones that don't. This is a stunning new field of research, and when applied to quantum state tomography is able to efficiently reconstruct the state of a system not only accurately according to data, but realistically such that they obey the laws of quantum physics.

Quantum state tomography reminds us that even in a world ruled by uncertainty, if we learn how to look patterns may be born from the chaos. Piece by piece, nibble by nibble, we may be able to pull back the curtains and stare into the black box that is the quantum realm. In doing this, we not only get closer to perfecting quantum information systems, but also accelerating the field of physics informed machine learning.
